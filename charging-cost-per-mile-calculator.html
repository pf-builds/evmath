How this is calculated

Cost per mile for the EV is your electricity rate divided by miles per kWh (or your rate multiplied by kWh per 100 miles, divided by 100). Monthly cost multiplies that by your entered mileage. The gas comparison, if enabled, divides your local gas price by the vehicle's MPG the same way.

Where these numbers come from

Vehicle efficiency ratings (miles/kWh, kWh/100mi, and MPGe) are published by the EPA and DOE at fueleconomy.gov for every EV sold in the U.S. Average residential electricity rates and gasoline prices are tracked by the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA). This calculator doesn't use a fixed national number for either, since local rates vary too much to be useful, you enter your own.

Why real-world cost differs from the label

EPA efficiency ratings are measured under test conditions. Cold weather, highway speeds, climate control, and charging losses between the wall and the battery all reduce real-world efficiency, often by 10-30%. Your vehicle's trip computer or a charging app's session history will give a more accurate efficiency number than the window sticker.

What this doesn't account for

This models home charging at a flat rate. It doesn't account for time-of-use electricity pricing, the higher per-kWh cost typical of public DC fast charging, demand charges, or charging equipment cost. See the home charging payback calculator for the public-vs-home cost question.

Frequently asked

How do I find my actual electricity rate?

Check a recent electric bill for the total price per kWh. Some utilities show one blended rate, others break it into generation and delivery charges that need to be added together. If your utility offers time-of-use pricing, use the rate for whenever you'd actually charge, off-peak overnight rates are often significantly lower and worth checking for. Your utility's website or customer portal usually has this listed per kWh.

How do I find my vehicle's efficiency?

The EPA rating in miles/kWh or kWh/100 miles is on the window sticker (Monroney label) or can be looked up for your exact trim at fueleconomy.gov. For a more accurate number, your vehicle's trip computer or a charging app's session history reflects your actual driving and climate, which is usually more accurate than the label rating.

Why does this use miles/kWh or kWh/100mi instead of MPGe?

MPGe converts electricity into a gasoline-equivalent unit for EPA label comparisons, but it doesn't translate directly into a dollar cost. Miles per kWh (or kWh per 100 miles) multiplied by your actual electricity rate gives a real cost per mile, which is what this calculator is built around.

Why might my real-world cost differ from the EPA rating?

EPA efficiency ratings are measured under test conditions. Cold weather, highway speeds, climate control, and charging losses between the wall and the battery all reduce real-world efficiency, often by 10-30%. Using your own trip computer or charging app history instead of the label rating will give a more accurate cost.

What doesn't this calculator account for?

This models home charging at a flat rate. It doesn't account for time-of-use electricity pricing, the higher per-kWh cost typical of public DC fast charging, demand charges, or any charging equipment cost. Use the home charging payback calculator for the public-vs-home cost question.
