How this is calculated

Monthly savings is your monthly charging volume multiplied by the difference between your public and home electricity rates. Breakeven is the charger and install cost divided by that monthly savings, the number of months until the install has paid for itself compared to continuing to charge publicly.

What drives install cost

The charger itself is commonly a few hundred dollars, but installation depends on how far the charger is from your electrical panel and whether the panel has enough spare capacity. A straightforward install can be a few hundred dollars; one requiring a panel upgrade can run well over $2,000. The U.S. Department of Energy's Alternative Fuels Data Center has general background on home charging equipment, but a local electrician's quote is the only way to get an accurate number for your home.

Finding your public charging rate

Check your charging network's app or past session receipts for the per-kWh (or per-minute) price you were charged. Public charging pricing varies by network, location, and membership tier, and some networks price by time instead of energy, so use your own recent sessions rather than a general assumption.

What this doesn't account for

This doesn't account for electricity rate changes over the years, any panel upgrade cost beyond what you enter, or utility EV charging incentive rates and rebates that some utilities offer. It also doesn't weigh the convenience of public fast charging against the slower overnight charging a home Level 2 charger provides, which is a real factor beyond the dollar cost.
