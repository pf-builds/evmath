The capacity-loss rates and per-kWh replacement cost used here are wide, general-industry approximations assembled from publicly reported EV fleet degradation research (studies of the kind published by Geotab and Recurrent Auto) and typical battery replacement cost reporting. They are not manufacturer-specific or model-specific figures. If you're making a real financial decision, get a battery health check from a dealer or independent EV shop and a specific replacement quote.

How this is calculated

Capacity retained is estimated from a per-year decline range applied to your vehicle's age, wider for NMC/NCA chemistry, narrower for LFP, and widest if chemistry is unknown. Replacement cost multiplies your pack size in kWh by a wide dollar-per-kWh range covering parts and labor. Both are shown as ranges on purpose, a single precise number would overstate what public research actually supports for an individual vehicle.

Why chemistry matters

LFP (lithium iron phosphate) packs are broadly reported in published research to tolerate more charge cycles and degrade more slowly than NMC or NCA packs, though typically at lower energy density for the same physical size. If you don't know your vehicle's chemistry, the estimate widens to responsibly cover both rather than guessing.

Why replacement cost varies so much

Replacement cost depends on pack size, whether the whole pack or just a module needs replacing, manufacturer versus independent labor rates, and current battery material pricing, which fluctuates over time. Get a quote from a dealer or a qualified independent EV shop for a number specific to your vehicle.

What this doesn't account for

Most EV batteries sold in the U.S. carry a minimum 8-year/100,000-mile warranty under federal requirements, which may cover a failing pack outright, check your specific vehicle's coverage before assuming a replacement cost applies to you. This also doesn't model partial module-level repairs, which can cost far less than a full pack replacement on packs designed to allow it, or the effect of climate and DC fast charging habits, both of which can meaningfully speed up degradation beyond these general ranges.
