How this is calculated

Capacity retained applies a per-year decline rate to your vehicle's age. Geotab's 2026 study of 22,700+ EVs found average degradation of about 1.5% per year for vehicles charged mostly on AC power, rising to about 3.0% per year for vehicles that use DC fast charging frequently, with an average of 2.3% per year and roughly 82% capacity retained after 8 years across the full fleet. This calculator anchors its range to those two reported figures based on your selected charging habits. Replacement cost multiplies your pack size in kWh by a dollar-per-kWh range derived from Recurrent Auto's real-world reported installed costs, which run from about $4,000 for smaller packs to over $20,000 for larger ones.

Why charging habits, not chemistry, drive this estimate

Published fleet research measures degradation by charging pattern, not by chemistry, so that's what this calculator's range is anchored to. Chemistry still matters in general, LFP (lithium iron phosphate) packs are broadly reported to tolerate more charge cycles than NMC or NCA packs, but there isn't public fleet-scale data breaking degradation down by chemistry the way there is for charging habits. Rather than invent a chemistry-based number without a source, this calculator leaves chemistry out of the math.

Why replacement cost varies so much

Recurrent Auto's data shows installed replacement cost scaling with pack size and vehicle class, from about $4,000 for smaller packs to over $20,000 for larger ones, which implies a per-kWh range this calculator applies to your own pack size. Whether the whole pack or just a module needs replacing, and local labor rates, both push the real number around further. Get a quote from a dealer or a qualified independent EV shop for a number specific to your vehicle.

What this doesn't account for

Most EV batteries sold in the U.S. carry a minimum 8-year/100,000-mile warranty under federal law (Clean Air Act Section 177), which may cover a failing pack outright, check your specific vehicle's coverage before assuming a replacement cost applies to you. Recurrent Auto's research also found only about 2.5% of EVs in their tracked fleet have ever needed a replacement, so treat a full replacement as the less likely outcome, not the default one. This also doesn't model partial module-level repairs, which can cost far less than a full pack replacement on packs designed to allow it, or the effect of climate, both of which can shift real-world results beyond these ranges.
